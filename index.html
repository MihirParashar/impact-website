Research Overview

In recent years, carbon emissions have become increasingly dangerous to Earth's ecosystem and environment. Consequently, countries around the world have been putting in measures to counteract excessive carbon emissions and help reduce carbon emissions. However, access to these technologies is expensive, and low-income areas may not be able to reduce their carbon footprint.

Understanding the correlation between household income and carbon footprint is crucial to reducing inequalities and creating sustainable communities, fulfilling the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 11.

Understanding Climate Change

What Is Climate Change?

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns. These changes may be natural, but since the 1800s, human activities have been the main driver of climate change, primarily due to burning fossil fuels like coal, oil, and gas, which produces heat-trapping gases.

The Climate Change Debate

While there is a strong scientific consensus that climate change is happening and is primarily human-induced, there remains debate about the extent of human contribution versus natural climate variability. Our research acknowledges this discourse while focusing on actionable data that can inform policy regardless of one's position in this debate.

Research Methodology

Research Question

What is the correlation between average household income and carbon footprint across different geographical areas?

This research aims to investigate whether lower-income areas have higher average carbon footprints due to factors such as:

  • Inefficient housing and appliances
  • Reliance on older, less fuel-efficient vehicles
  • Limited access to renewable energy sources
  • Reduced ability to invest in energy-efficient upgrades
  • Geographical constraints and infrastructure challenges

Argument for Positive Correlation

  • Higher income families use more utilities
  • More expensive gas cars like Lamborghinis
  • More purchasing power for carbon-emitting vehicles and devices

Possible Negative Correlation

  • More money for eco-friendly goods
  • Electric cars and solar panels
  • Modern technologies designed to be eco-friendly

Research Results

Key Findings

Our analysis revealed several significant patterns in the relationship between household income and carbon footprint:

  • Income-Emission Relationship: The Pearson correlation between household income and carbon emissions was found to be -0.1295, indicating a negative linear correlation with a p-value of 1,408e-103, suggesting a statistically significant relationship of higher household income associated with slightly lower carbon emissions.

  • Geographic Variations: CO2 Per Household decreases in this order across the United States:
    Midwest - 0.1022 tCO2e/year
    West - 0.0861 tCO2e/year
    South - 0.0686 tCO2e/year
    Northeast - 0.0640 tCO2e/year
    This suggests that geographic factors play a significant role in carbon emissions, especially in areas with large transportation sectors and energy-intensive economies.

  • Contributing Factors: Our data doesn't directly show factors causation, but there are some variables we can infer:
    Low income areas tend to have older housing stock, which is less energy-efficient, and lower access to public transportation, leading to higher reliance on personal vehicles.
    Higher income areas tend to have more access to renewable energy sources and energy-efficient appliances, which contribute to lower carbon footprints.

Unexpected Discoveries

Several unexpected patterns emerged during our analysis:

  • Finding 1: The weak negative correlation between household income and carbon footprint at the household level is an unexpected discovery, but still supports our hypothesis. Conventional assumptions often suggest a positive correlation.
  • Finding 2: The top 10 highest carbon-emitting cities on average had around ~15k population with middle-to-low-class incomes, which differed from our expectation of high population cities to emit more on average.
  • Finding 3: The significant regional variations in household carbon footprints, with the Midwest showing the highest average, as variation between region wasn't expected to play as large a role as it did.

These findings challenge conventional assumptions about the income-emissions relationship and suggest new avenues for research and intervention.

Policy Implications

Our research has several important implications for policy development:

  • Targeted Interventions: Policies should be tailored to address the specific factors driving higher emissions in low-income areas.
  • Equity-Focused Approach: Carbon reduction efforts should prioritize accessibility and affordability to avoid exacerbating economic disparities.
  • Infrastructure Investment: Public investment in sustainable infrastructure should prioritize underserved communities.
  • Educational Initiatives: Community-based education programs can promote cost-effective carbon reduction strategies.
  • Data-Driven Decision Making: Ongoing monitoring of income-emissions relationships should inform policy adjustments.
