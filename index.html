Abstract

In recent years, carbon emissions have become increasingly dangerous to Earth’s ecosystem and environment. Consequently, countries around the world have been putting in measures to counteract excessive carbon emissions and help reduce carbon emissions. However, access to these technologies is expensive, and low-income areas may not be able to reduce their carbon footprint. Understanding the correlation between household income and carbon footprint is crucial to reducing inequalities and creating sustainable communities, fulfilling the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 11. This research project aims to determine the correlation, if any, between the average household income and the average carbon footprint of a geographical area. It is hypothesized that lower-income areas will have higher average carbon footprints due to factors such as inefficient housing, reliance on fossil fuels, and limited access to sustainable infrastructure. The research will utilize publicly available datasets on household income and carbon emissions by county and ZIP code. Using the results, a solution can be proposed to decrease the overall carbon footprint by specifically targeting the areas with a higher carbon footprint through cheap, preventative software.

Introduction

This project was created by a team of high school students at the Illinois Mathematics and Science Academy interested in mitigating the effects of climate change. These students have taken an interest in environmental protection and health from a young age. Given the opportunity, they chose to pursue this project regarding the exploration of climate change in a substantive and multilayered manner.
Climate change refers to the long-term increase in global average temperature and changes in weather patterns. While Earth's climate has varied naturally over millions of years, the new era of rapid warming began in the late 19th century, during the Industrial Revolution. That's when carbon dioxide (CO₂) emissions skyrocketed as factories and cars began spewing greenhouse gases into the atmosphere at unprecedented levels. Scientific consensus confirms that these emissions are trapping heat and accelerating global warming, which is causing sea levels to rise and weather events to become more extreme. This can have a disastrous effect on the environment by increasing the frequency of natural disasters and making habitats uninhabitable for many species. Research proves that climate change is directly influenced by human activity, especially through carbon emissions.
This project aims to help researchers understand how economic factors influence carbon emissions over geographical areas. We intend to explore a potential correlation between the average household income nationwide in the United States and the average carbon emissions in this domain.
